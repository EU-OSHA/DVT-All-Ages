About the visualisation tool     

Please read the following remarks about each section of the tool for a better understanding of the analysis carried out and the data sources considered.

Introduction to the project

The European Commission concluded a delegation agreement with the European Agency for Safety and Health at Work (EU-OSHA) to carry out a pilot project entitled ‘Safer and healthier work at any age — occupational safety and health (OSH) in the context of an ageing workforce’.

This was based on a decision and request of the European Parliament, whose objective was to further investigate possible ways of improving the safety and health of older people at work, as the ageing of the workforce will have a serious financial impact on workers, employers and taxpayers.

Thus, the project aimed to assess the prerequisites for OSH strategies and systems to take account of an ageing workforce and improve risk prevention and health protection for all throughout working life.

The project:

  • reviewed current knowledge in relation to OSH, ageing, and older workers;
  • investigated EU and Member States’ policies and strategies addressing the challenges of an ageing workforce, in particular in relation to OSH;
  • investigated EU and Member States’ policies and systems in relation to rehabilitation and return to work;
  • gathered examples of successful and innovative practices in workplaces.

Within the project, existing data and information were reviewed through desk research, including by national experts, and discussion groups in selected countries. The information obtained was summarised in various reports, including 31 country-specific reports, analyses of policies addressing the ageing of the workforce in the field of OSH, employment, public health and education, as well as rehabilitation and return-to-work systems and programmes.

This data visualisation tool presents the key findings of the project and allows users to explore the data in an interactive way by comparing countries and searching for policies, strategies and programmes, among other possibilities.

Data sources used in the project included harmonised data at the European level to ensure comparability, such as:

  • Eurostat — European Union Labour Force Survey (EU LFS), including ad hoc modules
    • The EU LFS is a large household sample survey providing quarterly results on the labour participation of people aged 15 years and over, as well as on persons outside the labour force. The data collection covers 1983 onwards. In general, data for individual countries are available, depending on their accession date.
    • EU LFS ad hoc modules — since 1999, the so called ‘ad hoc modules’ have been an inherent part of the EU LFS. The aim of the ad hoc modules is to provide users with data on a specific topic concerning the labour market. The following ad hoc modules were used in the project:
      • 2013 — accidents at work and other work-related health problems;
      • 2012 — transition from work into retirement.
  • Eurostat: European Statistics of Income and Living Condition (EU-SILC) survey
    The European Statistics of Income and Living Condition (EU-SILC) survey contains a small module on health, including 3 variables on health status: self-perceived health, chronic morbidity (people having a long-standing illness or health problem), and activity limitation – disability
  • Eurostat statistics on demography, economy, employment, and health.
  • European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions (Eurofound) — sixth European Working Conditions Survey (EWCS), 2015.
    The EWCS provides an overview of working conditions in Europe, including:
    • employment status, working time duration and organisation, work organisation, learning and training, physical and psychosocial risk factors, safety and health, work–life balance, worker participation, earnings and financial security, and work and health.
  • EU-OSHA — European Survey of Enterprises on New and Emerging Risks (ESENER), 2014
    • EU-OSHA’s ESENER is an extensive survey looking at how safety and health risks are managed in European workplaces, focussing on:
      • general OSH risks and how they are managed;
      • psychosocial risks, such as stress, bullying and harassment;
      • drivers of and barriers to action in OSH management;
      • workers participation in OSH.
    • The respondents to ESENER are those who know the most about safety and health at work in their enterprise (in small and medium-sized enterprises, this is generally the owner/manager; in large enterprises, it is generally an OSH specialist).
  • Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) data
    Statistics on average effective age of retirement
    • One indicator of retirement behaviour other than the more general factors affecting the level of participation in the labour market is the average effective age at which older workers withdraw from the labour force —referred to as the effective age of retirement.
  • European Commission — Special Eurobarometer 393 — Discrimination in the EU, 2012.
    • The survey provided an insight into the evaluation of perceptions, attitudes, knowledge and awareness of discrimination in the European Union.

The data visualisation tool provides background information for EU-OSHA Campaign 2016-2017, ‘Healthy Workplaces for All Ages’.

Key issues

Five interactive dashboards have been configured to present the key issues:

  • Health is an important factor for leaving the labour market.
  • Factors influencing older workers’ labour market participation.
  • Impact of work on health.
  • Health inequalities.
  • Dissatisfaction of older workers with working conditions.

Each dashboard has a main indicator that shows the key issue and the situation in countries and, next to this, several contextual indicators.

    • Country groups

    The country groups are based on the analysis of selected qualitative indicators related to the scope and overall orientation of the policies implemented to address the challenges of an ageing workforce; the level of integration and coordination across policy areas and stakeholders; and the implementation of policies. The countries have been grouped according to their approach to the issue, considering the following criteria:

    • policy priorities — Whether or not addressing workforce ageing is a priority for policy development ;
    • policy scope — Scope of policies addressing population ageing - OSH, employment, public health, education and equal treatment; the level of integration of policies; and the existence of formal coordinating structures across policy areas
    • concepts — Concepts used in policy development - sustainable work, well-being at work, sustainable employability, healthy ageing, active ageing, work ability, etc.
    • implementation — the coordination of stakeholders, social dialogue, evaluation of policies, etc.

    The country groups also reflect similarities and differences in terms of contexts, such as the magnitude and timing of population ageing and the economic situation, as well as indicators related to working conditions, labour market participation and health outcomes, illustrated by facts and figures:

    • Median age time series (Eurostat, 1994-2014), presenting a historical approach.
    • Unemployment rate (Eurostat — LFS, 2000-2015), focusing the analysis on the economic crisis period.
    • Older workers employment rates (55-64) (men/women) (Eurostat — LFS, 2000-2014), focusing the analysis on the economic crisis period.

    Country profiles

    This section provides country specific information on initiatives to address the ageing of the workforce. Included in the country cards are the main policies, strategies and programmes in the field of OSH and rehabilitation and return to work, or other relevant policies in the fields of employment, public health and education, as well as workplace practices that could be linked to specific policy initiatives.

    For each country, you will find the following information:

    • Relevant facts and figures.
    • The cluster to which the country belongs (please see section on ‘Country groups’ for more information).
    • Examples of OSH and related policies, strategies and programmes.
    • Other initiatives undertaken by the country that have an impact on the issue.
    • Special circumstances to be considered.
    • Future challenges, considering the country’s situation at the time of the research.
    • A reference to the country’s OSH system and stakeholders.
