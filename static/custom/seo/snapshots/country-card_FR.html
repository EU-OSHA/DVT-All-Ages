Main Policies, Strategies and Programmes

OSH policies

2008 law on funding social welfare for 2009

This law included an obligation for company agreements on the employment of older workers. Companies with 50 or more employees that are not covered by a sectoral or company agreement on this issue are required to conclude such an agreement at company level. All agreements must cover at least three of the following six dimensions:
  • Recruitment of older workers
  • Anticipation of career development
  • Improvement of working conditions and risk prevention
  • Skill development and access to training
  • Facilitating the work-to-retirement transition
  • Knowledge and skill transfer and the development of tutorship programmes.

OSH policies

The Workplace health plan for 2016–2020 (troisième Plan santé au travail – PST 3)

On 8 December 2015, the government and all representative social partners at national level adopted the third Workplace health plan. The plan has two key objectives:
  • Developing a risk prevention culture by:
    • continuing to work on a variety of ‘classic’ risks (e. g. falls and trips, chemical risks);
    • taking into account organisational risks (especially burnout or professional exhaustion syndrome);
    • anticipating emerging risks (e.g. carcinogens, endocrine disruptors, etc.)
  • Improving quality of working life through the mobilisation of all stakeholders in favour of prevention, so that working conditions are considered a strategic priority for companies.
    • promoting quality of working life
    • keeping workers with health problems in employment
    • reinforcing links between workplace and public health
  • Reinforcing social dialogue: the plan also emphasizes the role of social dialogue in workplace health policies and encourages the conclusion of collective agreements at sector and corporate level.
The Plan lays the emphasis on work ability and the need to retain workers with reduced capacity – not limited to workers with recognised disability – on the labour market. It sets as a priority to strengthen individual support for workers and early identification of workers potentially at risk of exclusion from the labour market. One of the objectives is to improve coordination between different actors (physician / occupational physician / social security), and anticipate the return to work earlier than it currently is.

Other initiatives

Guidance on “Return to work after cancer”

The guidance targets cancer patients who want to return to work, occupational health specialists, HR managers and any other relevant actor in the return to work process.
View detail

3rd annual report of the Societal Cancer Observatory

The report presents the results of the Observatory’s research on return to work after a cancer. The research describes cancer patients’ pathway and the obstacles that prevent them from staying at or returning to work. The report underlines the communication problems inside companies, as the occupational physician is rarely approached by employees, and the limited use of the pre-reinstatement visit that could be an important step to recreate the link with the work environment and to define the necessary changes before the employee returns to work.

Context

  • France has a moderately old population, and will experience a very moderate pattern of ageing from 2040.

  • A high proportion of older workers in France (47.1 %) reported work-related health problems in 2013 compared with the EU average (11.4 %). Work-related psychosocial health problems are comparatively more frequent, maybe as a result of the higher proportion of service-related occupations, in France than in the EU generally.

  • The concept of arduous work is incorporated in French labour law.

  • According to the ESENER results, France has medium composite scores, as regards the number of OSH management measures, which might be explained by the challenge of accommodating the requirements of process-based participatory OSH management when transposing the Framework Directive.

  • Historically, occupational medicine and occupational physicians have played a central role. There is a relatively high number of labour inspectors. The role of trade unions and social dialogue in the labour market and in health and safety is significant, and health and safety committees are an important element of OSH management.

Pending Challenges
  • The emphasis on middle age groups is insufficient in the current legal and policy framework, and most measures come too late in the worker’s career to be effective and ensure a sustainable working life. A life-course approach to career management, professional mobility, training and the diversity of career paths, is needed for keeping workers in employment for longer

  • The main issues preventing an effective approach to sustainable working lives are the fragmentation of the legal and policy initiatives, the lack of coordination among various actors in the field and the limited number of awareness-raising and dissemination activities.

  • The complexity of the current system leads to a lack of awareness of existing instruments, tools and initiatives.

  • There are concerns about a dual labour market being created, with an increasing number of workers who are significantly exposed to risks and for whom existing prevention mechanisms would be irrelevant or inapplicable.

OSH System and Stakeholders
  • The French OSH system is composed of two major public authorities: the General Labour Directorate of the Ministry of Labour, responsible for labour policy, and the Ministry of social affairs and health, responsible for social security services.

  • Occupational health services: employers are obliged to set up an internal company occupational health service if they have more than 500 employees or to subscribe to an intercompany service if they employ fewer than 500 employees or do not want to create an internal service.

  • Intercompany services are non-profit bodies structured into one or more geographical, and sometimes trade, sectors. They are funded by their member companies. The working teams are composed of occupational physicians, authorised occupational risk prevention officers (IPRPs) and nurses. The independent and inter-enterprise occupational health services are regulated in French Law No. 2011-867 of 20 July 2011 on the organisation of occupational health care.

  • This model is characterised by a variable trade union and state influence and a high proportion of expenditure on old-age pensions, reflecting the low labour-force participation of older workers and early retirement ages in this country.

  • The well-funded welfare system enables poverty reduction, high-quality health care and disability pensions, but one of its weaknesses is that it is sensitive to employment conditions and demographics.

  • While trade union density is very low in France (around 8 %), social dialogue is considered strong due to very high levels of collective-bargaining coverage (93 %). Collective bargaining at national and company level is very institutionalised and influential, in particular for working conditions and health and safety at work, which is also demonstrated by the large number of social partners’ agreements and initiatives in this area.
